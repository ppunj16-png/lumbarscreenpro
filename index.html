Straight Leg Raise (SLR) — Lasègue Sign
Sn 91%Sp 26%Radicular
▶
Patient Position

Supine lying with legs fully extended. Examiner stands on the ipsilateral side.

Procedure

With knee fully extended, examiner passively raises the leg by flexing at the hip. Raise slowly (1–2°/sec). Note the angle at which pain begins. Dorsiflex the foot at the painful angle to increase nerve tension. Normal: 70–90°. Positive: pain (radicular) reproduced between 30–70° and follows dermatomal distribution to the leg (below the knee).

Positive Finding

Reproduction of sciatic pain (sharp, electric, burning) radiating below the knee in a dermatomal pattern between 30–70° hip flexion. Local back pain only is NOT a positive SLR.

Clinical Significance

Implicates L4, L5, or S1 nerve root irritation (usually disc herniation compressing the nerve root). Highest sensitivity for lumbar disc herniation — useful to rule OUT disc herniation if negative.

Crossed SLR (Well Leg Raise)
Sn 29%Sp 88%Radicular
▶
Patient Position

Supine with both legs extended.

Procedure

Raise the UNAFFECTED (contralateral) leg. If this provokes radicular pain on the AFFECTED (ipsilateral) side, the test is positive.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of the patient's radicular pain on the symptomatic side when the asymptomatic leg is raised.

Clinical Significance

Highly specific for large central or paracentral disc herniation significantly compressing the nerve root. Low sensitivity but when positive, very suggestive of significant disc herniation (often large or central).

Slump Test
Sn 84%Sp 83%Radicular / Neural
▶
Patient Position

Sitting upright on the edge of the table with hands behind back.

Procedure (Sequential)

Step 1: Patient slumps thoracic and lumbar spine (full spinal flexion). Examiner applies overpressure at shoulders.
Step 2: Patient flexes the neck (chin to chest). Examiner applies overpressure.
Step 3: Passively extend the knee as far as possible.
Step 4: Add ankle dorsiflexion.
Step 5: Release neck flexion (look up). If pain resolves — neural tissue involved. If pain remains — likely musculoskeletal.
Positive: Reproduction of the patient's symptoms at any step that resolves or reduces with neck extension.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of radicular symptoms (pain/numbness down leg) during sequential loading, that is relieved when cervical spine is extended (sensitising manoeuvre released).

Clinical Significance

Excellent sensitivity and specificity for neural tissue involvement. Better than SLR in sitting patients. Useful for detecting adverse neural tension (ANT) from disc herniation, neural adhesions, or piriformis entrapment.

Femoral Nerve Stretch Test (FNST) / Prone Knee Bend
Sn 84%Sp 83%Radicular (L2–L4)
▶
Patient Position

Prone lying with examiner on ipsilateral side.

Procedure

Passively flex the knee, bringing heel toward buttock. Then passively extend the hip slightly. Hold for 5 seconds. Positive: anterior thigh/groin pain reproduced radiating in an L2–L4 dermatomal distribution.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of anterior thigh, medial leg, or groin pain (L2, L3, L4 distribution). Local knee pain or back pain alone is NOT positive.

Clinical Significance

Tests upper lumbar nerve roots (L2, L3, L4). Disc herniation at L2-L3 or L3-L4 levels. Differentiates upper from lower lumbar involvement (SLR tests L4–S1).

Bowstring Test (Cram Test)
Sp 89%Radicular
▶
Patient Position

Supine. Perform SLR first to the angle of pain onset.

Procedure

At the angle where pain is reproduced during SLR, flex the knee slightly (20°) to reduce tension. Then apply firm digital pressure into the popliteal fossa (directly on the sciatic nerve). A positive test reproduces the radicular pain.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of sciatic pain with direct pressure on the sciatic nerve in the popliteal fossa (confirming the nerve, not the hamstrings, is the pain source).

Clinical Significance

Highly specific for sciatic nerve root involvement. Helps differentiate true sciatica from hamstring tightness or referred musculoskeletal pain.

Valsalva Manoeuvre
Disc / Space-occupying
▶
Patient Position

Sitting upright.

Procedure

Ask the patient to take a deep breath and strain (like bearing down or attempting to expire against a closed glottis) for 10 seconds. Ask if this worsens or produces their pain/neurological symptoms.

Positive Finding

Reproduction or worsening of radicular pain or neurological symptoms (pain, numbness, tingling).

Clinical Significance

Increased intrathecal pressure compresses the irritated nerve root. Positive in disc herniation, spinal stenosis, or tumour. Also positive during coughing, sneezing. Contraindicated in patients with cardiovascular disease.

McKenzie Repeated Movement Assessment
MDTDirectional Preference
▶
Procedure

Repeated Lumbar Extension in Lying (REIL): Patient prone, pushes up on hands, repeating 10 times. Note effect on pain (centralisation / peripheralisation).

Repeated Lumbar Flexion in Lying (RFIL): Supine, knees to chest, 10 repetitions. Note effect on pain.

Repeated Lateral Flexion in Standing: Slide hand down thigh 10 times each direction.

Key: Does pain centralise (good — directional preference) or peripheralise (bad — avoid this direction)?

Positive Finding / Interpretation

Extension preference (derangement): Extension centralises/reduces pain → extension-based treatment.
Flexion preference (dysfunction/stenosis): Flexion reduces pain → flexion exercises.
Lateral shift: Side-glide correction first, then reassess.
Centralisation: Strongest predictor of good outcomes.

Clinical Significance

Classifies LBP into MDT subgroups: Derangement, Dysfunction, Postural syndrome. Directional preference guides exercise selection and is a strong positive prognostic indicator.

Quadrant Test (Kemp's Extension Quadrant)
Facet / Foraminal
▶
Patient Position

Standing. Examiner behind patient.

Procedure

With one hand on the patient's shoulder and the other providing counterpressure on the iliac crest, guide the patient into combined extension + lateral flexion + rotation to the SAME side (closing the facet joint and narrowing the foramen ipsilaterally). Apply gentle overpressure and hold 3 seconds. Repeat to the opposite side.

Positive Finding

Local back pain (facet dysfunction/impingement) or reproduction of radicular pain (foraminal stenosis/impingement) on the ipsilateral side.

Clinical Significance

Implicates ipsilateral facet joint pathology, foraminal stenosis, or nerve root impingement. Closing manoeuvre: stresses posterior elements.

Prone Instability Test
Sn 61%Sp 57%Instability
▶
Patient Position

Prone, with lower body hanging off the edge of the table (feet touching floor).

Procedure

Phase 1 (resting): Examiner applies posterior-anterior (PA) pressure over the lumbar spine. Note if pain is produced.

Phase 2 (active): Ask patient to lift both feet off the floor (activating lumbar extensors/stabilisers). Examiner re-applies PA pressure at the same level. If pain is now REDUCED or abolished — test is positive for instability.

Positive Finding

Pain produced in resting phase but reduced/abolished when muscles are contracted. Indicates segmental instability where muscle activation provides stability.

Clinical Significance

Positive test suggests lumbar segmental instability. These patients benefit greatly from motor control/stabilisation exercises (deep stabilisers — multifidus, transversus abdominis training).

Active Straight Leg Raise (ASLR)
Sn 87%Sp 94%Lumbopelvic Stability
▶
Patient Position

Supine with legs extended, feet 20 cm apart.

Procedure

Ask patient to actively lift one leg to 20 cm off the table without bending the knee. Score on 0–5 scale:
0 = No difficulty | 1 = Minor difficulty | 2 = Moderate difficulty | 3 = Major difficulty | 4 = Unable to do
Then compress the iliac crests laterally (manual compression) and repeat. If easier — confirms lumbopelvic instability (pelvic belt effect).

Positive Finding

Perceived heaviness, weakness, or inability to raise leg, particularly in post-partum women or with pelvic girdle pain. Improved with manual compression confirms instability.

Clinical Significance

Gold standard for posterior pelvic girdle pain and lumbopelvic stability assessment. Excellent sensitivity and specificity for Posterior Pelvic Pain (PPP) / Pelvic Girdle Pain (PGP).

Posterior-Anterior (PA) Spring Test
Segmental
▶
Patient Position

Prone lying. Examiner beside patient.

Procedure

Using thenar eminence or thumbs, apply a posterior-to-anterior (PA) pressure over each lumbar spinous process (L1–L5) and sacrum. Grade: Normal spring (some give), Hypomobile (stiff, rigid), Hypermobile (excessive give), Painful (provokes familiar pain).

Positive Finding

Reproduction of the patient's familiar pain with PA pressure (segmental dysfunction). Stiffness suggests hypomobility (manipulation/mobilisation candidate). Excessive spring suggests instability.

Clinical Significance

Identifies hypomobile or painful spinal segments. Guides level-specific spinal mobilisation/manipulation therapy. Can identify segmental instability at specific levels.

FABER Test (Patrick's Test)
Sn 77%Sp 72%SIJ
▶
Patient Position

Supine. Examiner on ipsilateral side.

Procedure

Place the test leg in Figure-4 position: hip Flexion, ABduction, External Rotation (FABER). Place the ankle just above the opposite knee. Gently apply downward pressure on the bent knee while stabilising the contralateral ASIS. Hold 5 seconds.

Positive Finding

Ipsilateral groin/anterior hip pain: Hip joint pathology (OA, labral tear).
Ipsilateral SIJ or buttock pain: SIJ dysfunction.
Contralateral SIJ pain: Contralateral SIJ involvement.
Compare distance of bent knee from table bilaterally.

Clinical Significance

Multipurpose test: screens for SIJ dysfunction, hip OA, hip labral pathology, and iliopsoas pathology. Also useful for comparing ROM bilaterally.

Sacral Thrust Test
Sn 53%Sp 72%SIJ
▶
Patient Position

Prone lying. Examiner beside patient.

Procedure

Apply a firm downward PA pressure directly over the sacrum (centre) using the heel of both hands. Apply a single brisk force (not oscillatory). Note if this reproduces the patient's familiar SIJ pain.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of familiar posterior pelvic pain / SIJ pain.

Clinical Significance

Part of the Laslett SIJ provocation cluster (≥3 of 5 tests positive = 91% diagnostic accuracy). Used in combination with distraction, compression, thigh thrust, and Gaenslen's tests.

Thigh Thrust Test (Posterior Shear)
Sn 88%Sp 69%SIJ — Best Single Test
▶
Patient Position

Supine. Hip flexed to 90° on the test side. Knee bent.

Procedure

Examiner places one hand behind the sacrum (as a fulcrum) and applies a posteriorly-directed (toward the table) force through the femur (axial load through the thigh). Apply 3–5 slow repeated compressions. Repeat on the other side.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of familiar posterior pelvic / SIJ pain ipsilaterally (NOT lumbar pain or groin pain).

Clinical Significance

Highest sensitivity of all SIJ provocation tests. The single best SIJ test. Part of Laslett's cluster. If negative, SIJ pain is unlikely.

Gaenslen's Test
Sn 71%Sp 26%SIJ
▶
Patient Position

Supine at edge of table. Affected hip hangs off the edge.

Procedure

Examiner flexes the unaffected hip and knee toward the chest (patient holds). The affected leg is allowed to hang off the side of the table into extension. Examiner applies additional extension overpressure to the dangling leg while stabilising the opposite flexed thigh. One SIJ is stressed in flexion, the other in extension simultaneously.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of familiar SIJ or posterior pelvic pain on the test side.

Clinical Significance

Stresses both SIJs simultaneously in opposite directions (torsion stress). Part of Laslett's SIJ cluster. Can also stress the hip joint and lumbar spine.

SIJ Distraction Test (Gapping)
Sn 60%Sp 81%SIJ
▶
Patient Position

Supine with legs straight.

Procedure

Examiner places both hands on the bilateral ASIS and applies a firm downward and outward pressure (distracting the SIJ anteriorly). Hold 30 seconds. Note if familiar SIJ pain is reproduced.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of familiar posterior SIJ pain (Note: some pain may be anterior — anterior ligament stretch).

Clinical Significance

Stresses the anterior SIJ ligaments. Part of Laslett's cluster for SIJ pain diagnosis. Better specificity than sensitivity.

Waddell's Non-Organic Signs (5 Signs)
Psychosocial
▶
Procedure & Signs

1. Tenderness: Superficial — non-anatomic tenderness to light touch (skin roll). Widespread — non-anatomic distribution of tenderness.

2. Simulation: Axial Loading — pressing down on the skull should not produce LBP. If it does = positive. Rotation — rotate shoulders and pelvis together — should not produce LBP.

3. Distraction: Compare seated SLR vs supine SLR. Patient with true organic pathology will be consistent. If significantly inconsistent = positive.

4. Regional Disturbance: Motor — non-anatomic weakness (cogwheel giving way). Sensory — non-dermatomal/stocking distribution sensory loss.

5. Over-Reaction: Disproportionate pain behavior (grimacing, sighing, bracing) compared to the mechanical stimulus.

Interpretation

≥3 positive signs indicate significant psychosocial contribution to pain. NOT a test for malingering/faking. Indicates need for biopsychosocial management, psychologist referral, pain education.

Kernig's Sign
Meningeal / Neural
▶
Patient Position

Supine.

Procedure

Flex hip to 90°, then attempt to extend the knee. Resistance or pain with knee extension (especially neck pain or radiating pain) is positive.

Positive Finding

Inability to extend knee fully due to pain or hamstring spasm, with possible radiating symptoms.

Clinical Significance

Originally described for meningeal irritation. In LBP context, implicates neural tension/irritation. Positive in both meningitis and significant disc herniation.

Piriformis Test (FAIR Test)
Piriformis Syndrome
▶
Patient Position

Sidelying with symptomatic side up, hip flexed ~60°, knee flexed ~60°.

Procedure

Examiner applies downward pressure on the knee (hip into internal rotation and adduction). This stretches the piriformis. Positive: reproduction of buttock/posterior thigh pain or sciatica-like symptoms.

Positive Finding

Deep buttock pain or radiating pain down posterior thigh/leg reproducing patient's symptoms. Distinguish from true disc sciatica (which should be negative in full SLR).

Clinical Significance

Suggests piriformis syndrome (sciatic nerve entrapment by piriformis muscle). Helpful when SLR is negative but patient has sciatic distribution pain.
